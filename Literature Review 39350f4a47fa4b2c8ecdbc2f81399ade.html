My project space

I’ve become very interested in indie game development recently, so I am wanting my project space to reflect this. To reflect the theme of smart /dumb interactions and the idea of ‘intelligence’ I’ve been researching the role of AI in games and how it can be applied to games in many different ways.

One area I've I've been looking at is the development of game worlds and 3D or 2D environments, and how these worlds could be generated by an AI. So, a lot of the research centres around generative landscapes and worlding. Worlding is a concept I looked at quite a lot last year too, so I would love to expand on this and create prototypes and projects using this as a central idea, whilst also being within the realm of indie game design.

As well as all the contents of this literature review, I've been trying to make an effort to learning how to use programs such as Unity and Blender to help me create these prototypes and in the end the final outcome. Starting out, I had a small amount of experience in Blender and none in Unity so this has also been a fairly big part of what I've been researching for this module also, so I'm able to actually start potentially create things in the future.

In this literature review, I have referred more to smart and dumb software than to smart and dumb objects. Whilst I've tried to include a few references to smart objects, they don't fit with the rest of my project area which is instead more about software and programs than the physical objects themselves.

Noise and Procedural generation

Creative AI: Procedural generation takes game development to new worlds

This article describes the artificial intelligence called ANGELINA. This AI has written gone from writing articles where it had a particular fondness for Rupert Murdoch, to participating in game jams using Unity and procedurally creating games. The article discusses how AI could be the next big step in the games industry, as AI becomes more and more competent at creating whole, sensical games which aren't restricted by human qualities such as fatigue, emotional needs and with potentially less bias.

"In a world where most current genres and core gameplay mechanics have been around for years, refined again and again to a sharp point, an AI could bring something new to the table."

This article contains an interview with a Goldsmith's research associate, Michael Cook, which could be very useful in going forwards in my research.

"Procedural content generation feels a bit like violating laws of the universe – creating something from nothing, again and again," he continues. "Of course it's nothing like that really, but you get a rush from seeing something appear suddenly."

Procedurally generated environments are mentioned as a use of AI within game design, with a mention to Speedtree (a tool to create completely random trees) and the giant game No Man's Sky. An AI could also be used to make the game more fitted to the users tastes, for example if a user loves world exploration, then the game would create expansive worlds for this user to navigate whilst if a different user enjoyed this aspect of exploration less, the game would create a more linear world for them to have to navigate.

One thing to note is that in these AI generated games, they often lose the element of story telling.

"To me what I think is the big missing area in terms of game generation or automatic game design is the storytelling components, which doesn't have this nice physical manifestation that we can work on," Riedl says. "It's more abstract constructs and conceptual sorts of ideas."

I think this is an important thing I should keep in mind when starting to develop prototypes of my ideas as I could end up generating lots of cool looking but empty worlds.

The article talks of this use of AI within games being a mostly undeveloped field, which is exciting to me as whilst I don't have large amounts of technical capabilities to make this field expand I can watch it grow and potentially be a part of it in the future. Overall, I found this article very useful as it fits into my project scope very well, and helped in finding different resources and games to look at too.

Moss, Richard (2015) "Creative AI: Procedural generation takes game development to new worlds", New Atlas [Online] Available here: https://newatlas.com/creative-ai-procedural-game-development-angelina/35874/

Building Worlds with Noise Generation | No Man's Sky

Video from one of the main programmers of the game No Man's Sky discussing how the game utilises noise for world generation, and the process of creating these algorithms for this game as well as other general information about the game's creation.

Discusses the downsides to Perlin noise, for example it creating very repetitive features such as this mountainous landscape, which doesn't really mirror any real life mountains as 'the slope, the gradient is reasonably constant.'

They tried creating noise with real world data plugged into it to try and create a more realisitc looking landscape, however then came to the conclusion that this terrain they had generated was really unexciting.

"Noise, in the real world, is exponentially distributed" "However, it is unfortunate. The real world is super boring"

Different octaves of noise can have different prominence, eg the first octave doesn't need to be very prominent in the last calculation as this is what can often drive the extreme repetitiveness.

Speculative Before the Turn: Reintroducing Feminist Materialist Performativity

This paper was a reading that was initally for my Critical Studies class, but it has one section about Rotifers which I find particularly interesting. They are a type of bacteria that reproduce entirely asexually as the species has no living males in it and seemingly hasn't for around 80 million years, so the living females clone themselves in order to reproduce. The species import and copy DNA from other organisms it comes across, with studies showing they contain multitudes of foreign DNA from plants, fungi and other bacteria. They are also extremely

Whilst this concept isn't particularly relevant to AI, they could bring up questions about intelligence. Are these beings aware that they're completely female, are extremely durable and can import the DNA of other creatures? Probably not, or they'd probably have evolved human level intelligence at least by now

For me, the rotifer gives me inspiration to some creatures or mobs that could be part of the world, which opens up many possibilities. A gene-swapping, adaptive, entirely female species could be so many different things and would be an interesting group of entities to try and develop through code, with them swapping different genes and creating new creatures each time the code runs. The basic traits of rotifers have been used in science fiction previously, such as by Octavia Butler in her novels, and I feel like I could potentially include something similar in my project.

Overall, this article is about something completely different to the subtopic of Rotifers, and instead is what my Critical Studies seminar leader, Annie Goh, referred to as 'Academic Beef' between different philosophers. The topic of Rotifers in this article feels rather random and unrelated to the rest of the essay, as the rest is a discussion about the 'subject' and the 'object' within ontology, but despite this I am still glad to have learned about the existence of this odd species.

I spent a week making an AIs game idea - Sebastian Lague

This is a video in which Sebastian Lague created a game called Super Chore Man in the game engine Unity, with the use of AI being that the AI generated a random idea for a game. This video was very helpful in my understanding of Unity and how games like this are developed, despite me being no where near this level of skill in Unity as it depicts the use of particle systems, material textures and multiple shaders. However this video has no use to me in terms of potentially making or utilising an AI in Unity though, which could be where my project leads me in the future.

My main criticism of this video is that the use of AI is fairly minimal, with the one use being to generate a random idea to then create a game from. This added with the human choice of which AI generated idea to use out of many takes the video and game further and further from the artificial intelligence aspect. However, I do not think the point of the game is to be majority AI or use AI very much as it is merely there to help solve a creative block, so whilst this wouldn't be a good criticism of the video as a whole, in the context of my research I do think it's use of AI is limited. Despite this, I still thought the video was very useful in terms of increasing my understanding of Unity as a program and the concepts within games programming.

Games

Stone Story RPG

Stone Story is a game where an AI controls all the movement of the character. The user is still necessary and makes all the decisions outside of the movement, like which areas to go and what to do with inventory items, but they ultimately have no direct control of the character that they play as. This makes the game a part of the auto-RPG genre.

Quote from the game's steam page:

One of Stone Story's defining mechanics is that you have no direct control of the player character, as an A.I. does all the exploring, combat and looting. Though this is an auto-RPG, that doesn't mean it's an idle game. Potions and special abilities are maximized by good timing. Boss fights are intense, requiring quick item swaps if the boss changes patterns.

My criticism of this game from only playing the demo through myself is that when you are playing, it isn't evident that the thing controlling the game is an AI, it feels more like the character is directly programmed to do its tasks to complete the game in a random order instead of an AI deciding what to do in what order. All the tasks are so basic and its doesn't learn anything, so I'm not sure if it could technically be called intelligence, if its just following the same algorithm over and over again. But then, this thinking could also be applied to lots of other 'smart' devices or processes and brings back around the question of 'what is intelligence?'.

Despite this, I think its still a good resource for my project as an example of an AI within a game, and provides some inspiration for potentially adding more to my project than just a procedurally generated environment.

Stone Story RPG (2019) San Mateo, California, Martian Rex. Available from: https://store.steampowered.com/app/603390/Stone_Story_RPG/

Minecraft

This video above and sighted below describes the procedural generation to create stunning blocky worlds within the game Minecraft. Worlds are generated in chunks, 16 by 16 by 256 blocks pieces of the world, which all come together to create the entire world. Initial chunks are created when a player first enters the world, and then chunks are generated as player moves to create this entire world. Works again using a seed, and every separate biome in the game is generated slightly differently.

Something interesting about Minecraft generation I found in this video is the variable of temperature that affects the world generation. Each biome and altitude has a different value which affects a number of different things, such as if it rains or snows but also the colour of water, grass and leaves. It also restricts biomes of vastly different temperatures from generating next to each other. This variable has been very interesting to learn about as it could be very helpful in creating my own project.

Minecraft was a big inspiration of No Man's sky, as said in Sean Murray's presentation cited above. He said he essentially rebuilt Minecraft from scratch to gain an idea of how the landscape generation worked, in order to implement a similar method into his own product (Murray, 2017)

One criticism of Minecraft overall as a game would be its lack of overall story, narrative or compelling ultimate goal. There is one goal in the game, to kill the Ender dragon in the End dimension, however the game doesn't tell you to do this, and you can only learn of it if you research it outside of the game. It also is only a small quest, and after completion you're left in this expansive landscape with no other purpose than build what ever is in your imagination or explore. Whilst this can be fun for some time, it can get boring fairly quickly.
